Perception and Relative Velocity

Human perception is intimately connected to relative motion. As an observer's velocity increases with respect to the surrounding environment, the amount of information that can be perceived accurately tends to decrease. Fine details become blurred, reaction time becomes increasingly critical, and perception shifts toward the most prominent features of the scene. In this sense, perception is not solely a property of the observer, but also of the observer's motion relative to the world. From a physical perspective, the notion of complete rest is itself an idealization. Brownian motion demonstrates that microscopic particles are constantly jostled by molecules in thermal motion, revealing that matter is perpetually active at the molecular scale. Even an object that appears perfectly stationary is, internally, a dynamic system of atoms and molecules in continuous motion. Absolute rest exists only as an ideal limiting case, approached as temperature tends toward absolute zero. These observations suggest an intriguing hypothesis: perception may depend not on absolute velocity, but on the relative velocity between an observer and the surrounding environment across different scales. As this relative velocity increases, the observer receives information at a rate that may exceed the brain's capacity to process it, resulting in a progressive reduction in perceptual richness. From this viewpoint, perception could be modeled as a finite-bandwidth information system, where motion governs not only what is seen, but also how much of reality can be meaningfully experienced.
